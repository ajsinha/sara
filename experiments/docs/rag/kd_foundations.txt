Knowledge Distillation: Theoretical Foundations and Modern Practice

Knowledge distillation (KD) is a model compression technique that transfers learned
representations from a large, high-capacity teacher model to a smaller, more efficient
student model. The foundational work by Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean (2015) formalised the
idea of using temperature-scaled softmax outputs as "soft targets" for training.

The Temperature Mechanism

The temperature parameter T controls the entropy of the output distribution. At T=1
(standard softmax), the distribution is peaked around the most likely class. As T increases,
the distribution flattens, revealing the relative probabilities between non-top classes.
This inter-class similarity structure is what Hinton termed "dark knowledge" — it encodes
the teacher's learned relationships between concepts that hard labels (one-hot vectors)
cannot capture. For example, a teacher classifying images might assign 0.001 probability
to "cat" when the true label is "dog" but only 0.0001 to "car". This 10x ratio tells
the student that dogs and cats share visual features that cars do not.

The Combined Distillation Loss

The training objective combines two loss terms:

  L = alpha * T^2 * KL(student_soft || teacher_soft) + (1 - alpha) * CE(student_hard, labels)

The first term (KL divergence) measures how well the student matches the teacher's softened
output distribution. The T-squared factor is a gradient magnitude correction: because
softening the distribution reduces gradient magnitudes by approximately 1/T^2, this factor
restores them to the correct scale. Without T^2 scaling, the distillation signal would be
overwhelmed by the hard-label cross-entropy term at high temperatures.

The alpha parameter (typically 0.5-0.9) controls the balance. Higher alpha values prioritise
the teacher's distributional knowledge, while lower values anchor the student to the labeled
data. In practice, alpha=0.7 with T=4-8 provides a good starting point for classification.

Variants of Knowledge Distillation

Feature-Based Distillation (FitNets): Rather than matching output distributions, FitNets
(Romero et al., 2015) align the intermediate layer representations of teacher and student.
This requires a projection layer to map the student's thinner hidden states to the
teacher's wider ones. The advantage is that the student learns not just what to predict
but how to represent the input internally.

Attention Transfer: Zagoruyko and Komodakis (2017) proposed aligning the spatial attention
maps between teacher and student. Attention maps summarise where the model "looks" when
making predictions. By matching these maps, the student learns to focus on the same input
regions as the teacher, even with fewer parameters.

Relational Knowledge Distillation (RKD): Park et al. (2019) argued that structural
relationships between samples matter more than individual representations. RKD distils
pairwise distance and angle relationships, teaching the student to preserve the geometric
structure of the teacher's representation space.

Self-Distillation: Born-Again Networks (Furlanello et al., 2018) showed that distilling a
model into an identical architecture can improve performance. The student, despite having the
same capacity, benefits from the regularisation effect of soft targets. This challenges
the assumption that distillation only works when compressing to smaller models.

Progressive Distillation: For large capacity gaps (e.g., 70B to 3B), direct distillation
may lose too much information. Progressive distillation introduces intermediate "teaching
assistant" models: the 70B teacher distils to a 13B assistant, which then distils to the
3B student. Each step reduces capacity by a smaller ratio, preserving more knowledge.

Online Mutual Distillation: Zhang et al. (2018) proposed Deep Mutual Learning, where two
student networks teach each other simultaneously without a pre-trained teacher. Both start
from random initialisation and share soft targets during co-training. Despite neither being
individually stronger, the mutual knowledge exchange consistently improves both, suggesting
diverse predictions from different initialisations provide implicit ensemble distillation.

Modern Applications in LLMs

In the era of large language models, knowledge distillation has taken new forms. API-based
distillation uses a large model (e.g., GPT-4) as a teacher to generate training data for
smaller models, without requiring access to the teacher's weights or logits. This is
sometimes called "distillation through generation" — the student learns from the teacher's
natural language outputs rather than its probability distribution.

The key challenge in LLM distillation is that output distributions are over vocabularies of
32K-128K tokens, making full distributional matching expensive. Most practical approaches
use sequence-level distillation, where the student is trained to match the teacher's complete
output sequences rather than token-level distributions.
