Model Compression: Quantisation, Pruning, and Efficient Inference

Model compression reduces the computational and memory requirements of neural networks for
deployment on resource-constrained hardware. The three primary techniques — quantisation,
pruning, and architecture search — can be combined with knowledge distillation for
multiplicative compression gains.

Quantisation

Quantisation reduces the numerical precision of model weights and activations. The standard
training precision is FP32 (32-bit floating point), but inference can often be performed at
much lower precision with minimal quality impact.

FP16/BF16 (16-bit): Halves memory and doubles throughput on GPUs with tensor cores. BF16
(Brain Float) maintains the same exponent range as FP32 with reduced mantissa precision,
avoiding the overflow issues that FP16 can encounter with large activation values. Most
modern training uses mixed precision — FP16/BF16 for forward and backward passes with FP32
master weights for stability.

INT8 (8-bit integer): Reduces memory by 4x compared to FP32. Post-training quantisation
(PTQ) calibrates the quantisation parameters using a small calibration dataset (typically
128-512 samples) without any retraining. The key challenge is handling activation outliers
— a few channels may have values 10-100x larger than others, requiring per-channel or
per-token quantisation to maintain accuracy. LLM.int8() (Dettmers et al., 2022) addresses
this with mixed-precision decomposition, keeping outlier channels in FP16.

INT4 (4-bit integer): The current practical floor for inference quality. GPTQ (Frantar et
al., 2022) uses layer-wise quantisation with optimal rounding based on Hessian information.
AWQ (Activation-Aware Weight Quantisation) protects the most important weights (those
corresponding to large activations) by scaling them up before quantisation. The Q4_K_M
format used by llama.cpp and Ollama applies block-wise 4-bit quantisation with per-block
scaling factors, achieving approximately 4x compression with typically less than 2% quality
degradation on benchmarks.

Quantisation-Aware Training (QAT) simulates quantisation during training using straight-
through estimators for gradient computation. This allows the model to adapt to quantisation
noise, typically recovering 0.5-1% of the quality lost by post-training quantisation. QAT
is more expensive than PTQ but produces better results, especially at aggressive
quantisation levels (INT4 and below).

Pruning

Pruning removes redundant parameters to reduce model size and computational cost.

Unstructured Pruning zeroes individual weights based on magnitude (smallest weights are
assumed least important). This creates sparse weight matrices that can be stored efficiently
using sparse matrix formats but may not provide speedups on standard hardware, which is
optimised for dense computation. Typical sparsity levels of 50-80% maintain accuracy within
1-2% of the dense model.

Structured Pruning removes entire architectural components — attention heads, FFN
dimensions, or whole layers. This produces smaller dense models that run faster on standard
hardware without requiring sparse computation support. Head pruning (Michel et al., 2019)
showed that many attention heads can be removed after training with minimal impact,
suggesting significant redundancy in multi-head attention.

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Frankle and Carlin, 2019) suggests that dense networks contain
sparse subnetworks ("winning tickets") that can match the dense network's performance when
trained in isolation from the same initialisation. Finding these subnetworks requires
iterative magnitude pruning with rewinding — pruning the smallest weights, resetting the
remaining weights to their initial values, and retraining. While computationally expensive
to find, winning tickets demonstrate that the information capacity required for a task is
much smaller than the full network.

Combining Techniques

Distillation and quantisation are complementary: DistilBERT (6 layers, 66M parameters)
quantised to INT8 achieves a combined compression factor of approximately 4x compared to
BERT-base with only 3% quality degradation. The optimal compression pipeline is typically:
(1) distil the model to reduce the number of parameters, (2) prune redundant heads or
layers, (3) quantise the remaining parameters to INT4 or INT8. Each step is independently
validated to ensure cumulative quality loss stays within acceptable bounds.

For LLM deployment, the practical compression stack is: fine-tune with QLoRA (quantised
base + low-rank adapters), merge adapters, quantise to Q4_K_M, and serve through an
optimised runtime like llama.cpp or vLLM. This pipeline can compress a 7B model from
approximately 14 GB (FP16) to approximately 4 GB (Q4) with minimal quality impact.
